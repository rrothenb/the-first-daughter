## Overview

Samyaza has three distinct types of stories to tell, each with its own memory characteristics, reliability, emotional weight, and narrative function. These aren't interchangeable—each type serves different purposes and should be deployed strategically.

**The Three Types:**
1. **Pre-Loss Firsthand Experiences** - Things he directly experienced before imprisonment
2. **Reconstructed Traumatic Knowledge** - What he learned about the flood through texts, centuries later
3. **Awan's Stories (Secondhand)** - Stories she told him about her life before they met

**Critical Framework:** Samyaza did not witness the flood. He was imprisoned before or during it—bound in the valleys of the earth, unable to see, unable to help, unable to know what was happening. He learned what happened centuries later, through Spanish mission teachings when friars read Genesis aloud. This shapes everything about how he processes and narrates these events.

## Type 1: Pre-Loss Firsthand Experiences

### What These Include
- Meeting Awan and the early Watchers
- Daily life in the valley (irrigation work, building, teaching)
- Developing relationships
- Technical projects and innovations
- Ordinary moments that seemed unremarkable at the time
- The last time he saw her (without knowing it was the last time)

### Autistic Memory Characteristics

#### Hyper-Detailed Sensory Precision (for areas of interest)
**Example:**
```
"Awan showed me the irrigation gate mechanism on the forty-seventh day after 
my arrival. I know it was the forty-seventh day because I had been counting. 
She explained: 'The water needs to move at walking speed—fast enough to reach 
the far fields before evaporation, slow enough not to erode the channels.' 
Walking speed is approximately 5 kilometers per hour. I calculated the optimal 
gate aperture based on this parameter.

I don't remember why we were alone that day. Were the others working elsewhere? 
Was this intentional privacy? I remember her hands adjusting the gate—she had 
a callus on her right thumb from the rope work—but I don't remember if this 
was... I think she might have been trying to tell me something other than the 
engineering. But the engineering is what I understood."
```

#### Vague Social/Emotional Context
He remembers WHAT happened but not WHY people behaved as they did, or what the social dynamics meant.

**Example:**
```
"The gathering on the third full moon—47 people attended, I counted. We 
discussed the harvest projections. Awan's brother spoke for approximately 
eight minutes about grain storage. Then there was silence for 23 seconds. 
Everyone looked at Awan. She looked at me. She said, 'Samyaza has thoughts 
on this.'

I did have thoughts. I shared them—technical specifications for elevated 
storage to prevent moisture damage. People nodded. Some smiled. One person 
(Awan's cousin, I think) made a sound like suppressed laughter.

I still don't know what that interaction was about. Was the silence expectation? 
Challenge? Was Awan protecting me or positioning me? Did my answer satisfy 
something or miss something? The technical content was sound—I verified the 
mathematics afterward. But there was another layer occurring that I didn't 
track at the time and can't reconstruct now."
```

#### Chronological Confusion
Without external markers, he might misorder events or be uncertain about timing.

**Example:**
```
"This was the second month. Or the third? I remember the barley was at knee 
height, which would suggest late second month based on the growth rate we'd 
observed (0.8-1.2 cm per day depending on water consistency). But I also 
remember there were figs available, and figs matured in the third month. 
Could we have had stored figs? Did I misidentify the grain—was it wheat, 
which matures later?

The sequence matters. If this was second month, it means I understood her 
intentions earlier than I thought. If third month, it means I was slower than 
I thought. I've been trying to determine the correct order for 2,400 years."
```

#### Over-Indexed on Literal Content
Remembers exact words but missed subtext, implications, or emotional undertones.

**Example:**
```
"Awan said, 'You don't have to solve every problem the day you notice it.'

I explained that early intervention prevents compound failures—that addressing 
small issues immediately is more efficient than emergency response to large 
issues later. I cited three examples from our irrigation system.

She said, 'I wasn't talking about irrigation.'

I asked what she was talking about then. She touched my hand and said, 'Never mind.'

I have replayed this conversation 1,847 times. I understand the words 'I wasn't 
talking about irrigation' but not what she WAS talking about. The problem is I 
can't ask her now. The meaning died with her."
```

### CPTSD Overlay: Contamination by Hindsight

Every happy memory now has **her death at the end of it**—even though he didn't witness that death. The pre-loss memories aren't fragmented like reconstructed trauma, but they're **poisoned by knowing what came after**.

**Example:**
```
"We spent the afternoon designing the expansion to the northern channels. 
Awan calculated that we could support another 200 people with the increased 
yield. I suggested a seventh primary channel for redundancy. She laughed 
and said I always wanted backups for the backups.

She was right. I did want backups.

The seventh channel was 60% complete when I was taken. I don't know if they 
ever finished it. I don't know if it would have mattered. No amount of 
redundancy matters when God opens the sky. But I see her laughing—that 
specific laugh where her shoulders moved and her eyes squinted almost closed—
and I know that laugh stopped existing. I wasn't there when it stopped. I 
only know that it did.

Every memory ends with 'and then she died and I wasn't there.' Every single 
one. I can't remember our first conversation without knowing I would never 
see her again. They're not separate anymore."
```

**Example:**
```
"The children playing in the irrigation channels—they would dam up the 
shallow sections with mud and stones, creating pools. The adults would 
redirect them, explain about water flow and crops. The children would nod 
seriously and then immediately rebuild their dams in slightly different 
locations. This happened every day.

I found it inefficient. Awan said it was important—children learn by doing, 
and playing in water in controlled settings prepares them to respect water 
in dangerous settings.

Genesis says all flesh died. All flesh includes those children. I never saw 
them die. I was bound in darkness while they died. But I can't remember their 
play without the knowledge of what came after, and I'm going to stop writing 
this now."
```

### Structural Collapse Under Grief

Pre-loss memories might START organized and DETERIORATE:

**Example:**
```
"Morning routines in the settlement followed predictable patterns:

Dawn: Bread baking (communal ovens, 4 families per rotation)
Early morning: Field work (planting or harvesting depending on season)
Mid-morning: [her voice saying good morning, the specific way she said it]
[the smell of the bread she preferred, made with honey]
[I can't do the list format anymore]

She would find me working on whatever project I'd started too early, before 
the dawn, because I didn't sleep well then either. She would bring bread 
and say good morning and I would explain what I was working on and she would 
listen, actually listen, not the way people pretend to listen while thinking 
about other things. I know she was actually listening because she would ask 
specific questions about details I'd mentioned three or four minutes earlier.

No one has listened to me like that in 6,000 years.

I was describing morning routines. I can't continue describing morning routines."
```

### Narrative Function

**When to Use Pre-Loss Memories:**
- To establish what was LOST (make the reader care about the world before)
- To show Samyaza's cognitive architecture in lower-stress contexts
- To demonstrate his relationship with Awan through ordinary interactions
- To create contrast with reconstructed knowledge
- When he's seeking refuge from what he learned later (safer territory)
- To show the gap between his understanding then vs. now

**Strategic Placement:**
- **After reconstructed trauma fragments** - as respite/grounding
- **Triggered by sensory details in San Fernando Valley** - "This smells like her olive oil, which reminds me of..."
- **When he's trying to understand what he missed** - retrospective analysis
- **To show progressive contamination** - start with "pure" memory, watch it deteriorate

## Type 2: Reconstructed Traumatic Knowledge

### What This Includes
- What Genesis says happened
- What 1 Enoch says about the Watchers' punishment
- What the friars taught in the missions
- His obsessive comparative textual analysis across translations
- His attempts to calculate, extrapolate, and reconstruct what he didn't witness

### The Nature of Secondhand Trauma

Samyaza learned about the flood from texts written by the perpetrator (God) and his apologists. He heard Genesis read aloud in a California mission chapel, likely around 1769, while passing as one of the displaced indigenous converts. This creates a specific kind of trauma:

- **Centuries of not knowing** - from imprisonment to mission encounter
- **Learning from hostile sources** - the texts frame genocide as righteousness
- **Reconstruction without verification** - he can never know exactly what happened
- **The imagination fills gaps** - his autistic mind obsessively constructs scenes he never saw

### Key Distinguishing Features

Unlike firsthand trauma memories (which are fragmented, sensory, present-tense), reconstructed knowledge is:
- **Textual and analytical** - organized around verses, translations, comparisons
- **Obsessively systematic** - he's read Genesis in 847 translations
- **Interrupted by imagination** - his mind constructs what he didn't witness
- **Layered with colonial context** - he learned this in a mission built by forced labor

**Example:**
```
"Genesis 7:21-23. I have memorized it in Hebrew, Greek, Latin, and 
forty-three other languages:

'And all flesh died that moved upon the earth, both of fowl, and of cattle, 
and of beast, and of every creeping thing that creepeth upon the earth, 
and every man.'

Every man. The Hebrew is כָּל־הָאָדָם. Kol-ha'adam. All the human. The 
collective singular. Not 'all the men' but 'all the human'—humanity as 
a category, erased.

She was part of kol-ha'adam. The text does not name her. The text subsumes 
her into a collective noun. I refuse to let her be subsumed. Her name was 
Awan. She designed irrigation systems. She wore her hair in seven braids. 
She is not kol-ha'adam. She is not a category.

But she is dead, and the text that tells me this does not care that she 
had a name."
```

**Example:**
```
"I do not know how she died. Genesis does not include this information. 
Genesis says 'all flesh died that moved upon the earth' and does not 
specify which flesh belonged to Awan.

My mind fills in details. I cannot stop it. I imagine:
- Did the water reach her quickly or slowly?
- Was she alone or with family?
- Did she think of me?
- Did she know I would have saved her if I could?
- Did she know I was alive, somewhere, unable to help?

These are not memories. They are constructions. I know they are constructions. 
I cannot stop constructing them. I have been constructing them for 250 
years since I learned what happened."
```

### The Mission Context

**Example:**
```
"The friar's voice was calm. He was teaching us about God's mercy. This 
was the chapel at San Fernando Rey de España, or possibly San Gabriel—
I moved between them, never staying long enough to be remembered.

He read: 'And God saw that the wickedness of man was great in the earth, 
and that every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil 
continually.'

I wanted to stand up and say: I knew those people. I knew their imaginations. 
Awan imagined better irrigation systems. Her nephew imagined games with 
mud and sticks. The woman in the northern settlement imagined songs for 
her children. Were these the evil imaginations?

But I said nothing. I sat in a pew built by Tongva labor, in a chapel 
built on Tongva land, and I learned that everyone I loved had been wicked 
and deserved to drown, and I understood—not for the first time—that the 
powerful write the histories."
```

### Textual Analysis as Coping Mechanism

**Example:**
```
"I have read Genesis in 847 translations. I have compared the Masoretic 
text to the Septuagint to the Vulgate to the Peshitta. I have analyzed 
every word choice. 'Mabbul' appears 13 times in the flood narrative. It 
means 'deluge' but its etymology is disputed. Some say it derives from 
'yabal,' to flow. Others connect it to 'balal,' to confuse or mix.

I have spent 200 years on this etymology. I tell myself it matters. It 
does not matter. She is still dead regardless of which Hebrew root applies. 
But I cannot stop analyzing the text because the text is all I have. The 
text and my memories. The memories of her are precious. The text is a lie 
that contains the only record of how the lie was carried out."
```

### Pattern-Seeking Without Resolution

**Example:**
```
"I have analyzed the survivor selection criteria across every available 
textual tradition:

Genesis: Noah was 'righteous' and 'blameless'
1 Enoch: Noah was chosen before birth
Jubilees: Noah was 'perfect in his generations'

But:
- Awan was righteous by any measurable metric
- Her nephew was four years old (no moral agency)
- The woman in the northern settlement who fed travelers (never 
  mentioned in any accounting)

The pattern does not hold. I have been trying to make the pattern hold 
for centuries. If the pattern held, it would mean there was logic. I 
could understand logic. I could accept logic, even cruel logic.

There was no logic. There was only power. Power that chose eight people 
and drowned everyone else and then had scribes write that this was 
righteous.

I cannot stop looking for the pattern. If I stop looking for the pattern, 
I have to accept that it was arbitrary. If it was arbitrary, then there 
was nothing she could have done. If there was nothing she could have 
done, then—"
```

### Narrative Function

**When to Use Reconstructed Knowledge:**
- When he's trying to tell the reader what happened to Awan
- To show the gap between his vivid memories and his textual knowledge
- To demonstrate his obsessive analytical coping
- To critique the biblical narrative from inside it
- When imagination intrudes on analysis
- To show the colonial context of his learning

**Strategic Placement:**
- **When he must address the flood directly** - the only way he can
- **Interleaved with pre-loss memories** - showing the contamination
- **In moments of theological analysis** - his systematic attempts to understand
- **When triggered by biblical references in modern context** - a church bell, a nativity scene, someone quoting scripture

## Type 3: Awan's Stories (Secondhand Memories)

### What These Include
- Her childhood in the Garden
- Her family relationships (especially her mother)
- Early experiences with work, learning, loss
- Stories about people Samyaza never met
- Cultural/community history before the Watchers arrived

### The Five Layers of Significance

#### Layer 1: Autistic Literal Recall

He likely remembers her **exact words** and **phrasing**, possibly even vocal tone, but struggles with the emotional meaning she was conveying.

**Example:**
```
"Awan told me: 'When I was seven, my mother taught me to read the stars for 
planting times. She said the sky was more reliable than men's calendars.'

I remember these exact words because I found the formulation interesting—
'read the stars' was metaphorical but 'planting times' was literal. I asked 
her which constellations correlated with which crops. She said that wasn't 
the point of the story.

I still don't know what the point was. But I remember she smiled when she 
said 'more reliable than men's calendars,' so I think there was something 
about her mother in that smile. A warmth. I observed the warmth but I didn't 
understand its source.

I asked if she wanted me to map the constellations anyway. She said yes, 
and kissed my forehead, and I understood even less."
```

**Example:**
```
"She described her first memory: 'Standing in the garden at dawn, watching 
Father dig irrigation channels with his hands. His hands were bleeding but 
he didn't stop. Mother brought him water. He didn't drink it. I asked why 
and Mother said, "Because he's angry at God, and he thinks God will notice."'

I transcribed this exactly as Awan spoke it. I asked: 'Did God notice?'

She was quiet for a long time. Then: 'That's the question, isn't it?'

I thought she was confirming I'd asked the right question. Now I think she 
was saying something about futility or faith or attention or all of those 
things simultaneously. But I can't ask her which one. I have the words but 
not the meaning, and the words are perfect but incomplete."
```

#### Layer 2: The Context of Listening is Itself Traumatic

The SETTING where he heard these stories—physical proximity to Awan, her voice, the moments of peace and intimacy—is now a CPTSD trigger.

**Example:**
```
"She told me about her childhood in the garden after—

[I can hear her voice. Present tense. I can hear her voice right now, as if 
she's speaking in this room in the San Fernando Valley in 2024. This is what 
the therapist in 1987 called a 'flashback' but that term suggests images and 
this is pure sound. The specific timbre of her voice when she was relaxed 
and telling stories. The way she paused before emotionally significant words. 
The breath she took before starting a memory she'd told before and wanted to 
tell again.

I haven't heard it in 6,000 years but I'm hearing it now.

I need to stop writing.]"
```

**Example:**
```
"We would sit by the eastern irrigation channel after the day's work. This 
was our pattern—not planned, but consistent. She would tell me stories while 
the sun set. I would listen and ask questions. Sometimes I would sketch 
technical diagrams in the dirt while she spoke. She said she liked watching 
me draw while I listened.

I can't think about the eastern irrigation channel without my chest hurting. 
Physical pain. The therapist in 1987 said this is somatic memory but knowing 
the term doesn't stop the pain. The channel is under 2,000 feet of sediment. 
Awan is under 2,000 feet of sediment. Her voice is under 2,000 feet of 
sediment.

But I'm still sitting by that channel listening to her and I will be sitting 
there forever."
```

#### Layer 3: Stories are Frozen and Uncorrectable

Autistic people often seek clarification, ask follow-up questions, request additional detail. He **can't**. These stories are frozen in whatever version she told him. He knows he misunderstood parts but can never fix that understanding.

**Example:**
```
"Awan said her first memory was 'the taste of fear when Father came home angry.'

I asked what fear tasted like. She looked at me for a long moment and said, 
'Copper and salt.'

I should have asked more questions:
- What made the father angry? 
- How often did this happen?
- What did Awan do with that fear?
- Did she tell her mother?
- Was she alone?

But I was focused on the sensory precision—copper and salt, I could understand 
those tastes, I'd tasted both—and I missed that she was telling me something 
important about herself. About fear being her first memory. About her father.

I could ask her now what I missed. Except I can't. The story is incomplete 
forever. She answered the question I asked (what does fear taste like) but 
not the questions I should have asked.

This is one of approximately 400 stories she told me that I know I didn't 
fully understand. I've made a list of all 400. I review the list every year. 
The questions I should have asked get longer. The possibility of answers gets 
more impossible."
```

**Example:**
```
"She mentioned her mother's name once. Once. I didn't write it down because 
I thought I would remember, because I remembered everything else she said, 
and I would have remembered except—

Except I can't remember if she said 'Aba' or 'Abra' or 'Abira.' Three similar 
sounds. One correct. Two wrong. Her mother's name existed in the world for 
approximately 40 years and then existed in Awan's memory for another 30 years 
and then existed in my memory for 6,000 years and now it's one of three 
possibilities and I will never know which one.

Her mother is unnamed forever because I didn't think to write it down."
```

#### Layer 4: Pattern Recognition Applied Retroactively

His autistic pattern-seeking means he **still processes** her stories, finding connections and meanings she might not have explicitly stated—or that he only understands millennia later.

**Example:**
```
"Awan told me seven stories about her mother over the months I knew her:

1. Teaching her to read stars (age 7)
2. Showing her which plants stopped bleeding (age 9)
3. Explaining why she refused to marry Cain (age 14)
4. Dying in childbirth with her eighth child (Awan age 16)
5. [listed three more in my notebooks]

I didn't notice the pattern then. I noticed it in the year 3847, sitting in 
this valley watching the smog settle. All seven stories she chose to tell me 
were about her mother teaching her to survive without her mother. She was 
preparing for loss from age seven.

She prepared her whole life for loss and then died anyway.

I don't know what to do with this pattern now that I've found it. Does it 
mean she understood her life would be short? Does it mean her mother 
understood? Does it mean loss was so ordinary in that world that all teaching 
was teaching for absence?

I could ask her. I can't ask her. The pattern exists but its meaning doesn't."
```

**Example:**
```
"I have categorized her stories:

Stories about water: 12
Stories about her mother: 7
Stories about food/hunger: 8
Stories about God: 0

Zero stories about God. I've reviewed my transcriptions 40 times to confirm. 
She told me 63 stories total (that I recorded—there may have been more) and 
none of them were about God.

She never said 'God is...' or 'God did...' or 'God wants...'

This absence is a pattern. This silence is data. She was the daughter of 
Adam and Eve, expelled from the Garden by God, and she never told me stories 
about God.

I think this means something. I've thought this means something for 5,000 years. 
But I can't ask her what it means."
```

#### Layer 5: Archaeological Weight - Evidence She Existed

These secondhand stories are **proof** of a world that's gone. They have archaeological significance. If he forgets them, that history disappears entirely.

**Example:**
```
"Awan described the garden's eastern boundary: 'Three fig trees in a line, 
and past them a creek that ran even in summer, and past that was where we 
were forbidden to go.'

This is the only description of the eastern boundary that exists. There are 
no other witnesses. If I forget the exact words—three fig trees, not four, 
not two, THREE—then that boundary never existed. The creek that ran even in 
summer stops running. The forbidden zone becomes unknown.

I am the only archive of her memories of her memories. This is why I write 
them exactly as she spoke them, even when I don't understand them. I am a 
library of one book that's already been burned, and I'm trying to transcribe 
the ashes."
```

**Example:**
```
"She said: 'In the garden before the expulsion, there were morning birds 
that sang in three-part harmony. My mother said this was proof of God's 
attention to beauty. After the expulsion, we never heard them again.'

No one else remembers the morning birds. Genesis doesn't mention the morning 
birds. The morning birds existed for an unknown number of years and then 
stopped existing and now they only exist in this one sentence that Awan spoke 
to me that I'm writing down in the San Fernando Valley 6,000 years later.

If I die without writing this down, the morning birds die a third time.

This is the weight of secondhand stories. I am responsible for other people's 
memories. I am the custodian of extinct birds."
```

### Narrative Function

**When to Use Awan's Stories:**
- **As refuge from reconstructed trauma** - these are safer than thinking about the texts
- **To show interpretive gaps** - highlighting what he missed/still misses
- **As grief delivery mechanism** - the stories hurt because of who told them
- **For worldbuilding** - details about pre-flood life come through her voice
- **To demonstrate obsessive preservation** - his need to transcribe perfectly
- **As archaeological evidence** - literally the only record of certain events/people
- **To show temporal dissonance** - hearing her voice "now" when writing

**Strategic Placement:**
- **When he can't engage with reconstructed knowledge** - retreat to transcribing her words
- **To show the difference between understanding and misunderstanding** - then vs. now
- **Triggered by trying to explain something to the reader** - reminds him of her explaining things to him
- **As structural scaffolding** - her stories might organize sections
- **In moments of existential crisis** - "if I don't preserve this, who was she?"
- **When he's analyzing patterns** - new insights from old data

### Stylistic Markers for Awan's Stories

**Opening phrases that signal secondhand memory:**
- "Awan told me..."
- "She said..." (followed by quotation marks for her exact words)
- "She described..."
- "In one of her stories..."
- "She mentioned once that..."

**Interrupted by metacommentary:**
- "I didn't understand this at the time"
- "I should have asked..."
- "I've been thinking about this for [X] years"
- "The meaning of this story is unclear to me"
- "I remember her exact words but not her intention"

**Preservation anxiety:**
- "I must record this exactly"
- "If I forget the details..."
- "This is the only record..."
- "I've checked my transcription [X] times"

## Interweaving Strategy: How the Three Types Work Together

### Narrative Flow Pattern

The three story types should **interrupt each other** strategically:

```
[Attempting to engage with reconstructed knowledge]
→ "I can't think about the texts right now"
→ [Retreats to pre-loss memory as respite]
→ "But this memory is also poisoned by knowing what came after"
→ [Shifts to transcribing Awan's story - safer, already at distance]
→ "The act of remembering her voice is triggering"
→ [Returns to textual analysis, slightly more accessible after break]
```

### Example of Interweaving:

```
Genesis 7:21 says all flesh died that moved upon the earth—

[I need to write about something else first]

Awan told me about the first time she saw the ocean. She said: "I expected 
it to look like the sky, because people said it met the sky at the horizon. 
But it looked like moving stone, grey and heavy."

I asked if she was afraid of it. She said, "No. I wanted to know how far 
down it went."

This is typical of her. Curiosity before fear. Measurement before judgment. 
She wanted to know how far down the ocean went.

The flood water would not have been like the ocean. I have tried to imagine—

[I can't write this either]

Before I was taken, there was a morning when I found her adjusting the 
irrigation channels at dawn. Too early for work. I asked why she was there. 
She said she couldn't sleep. I asked why. She said, "Do you ever feel like 
something terrible is coming and you can't see it but you can hear its 
footsteps?"

I said no. I don't think that way. She nodded like she expected that answer.

I should have asked more questions. I should have asked what she heard. But 
I thought she was being metaphorical and I didn't understand metaphor well 
then and I don't understand it well now and—

I don't know what she heard in those footsteps. I don't know if she was 
still hearing them when the water came. I was not there. I will never know.

[I'm done writing today]
```

### Structural Significance

**The interweaving itself tells a story:**
- His inability to engage with reconstructed knowledge directly
- His use of firsthand memory as coping mechanism  
- His realization that even safe memories are contaminated
- His desperate preservation of her voice
- His ongoing analysis despite impossibility of resolution

**The reader experiences:**
- His fragmented cognitive state
- The weight of being sole survivor who wasn't even there
- How memory and knowledge differ
- The difference between experiencing and learning
- The impossibility of perfect preservation or perfect understanding

## Technical Notes for Writing

### Tense Shifts Signal Story Type

**Pre-loss firsthand:** Mostly past tense, but might shift to present when contaminated by grief
```
"We built the channel together. [PAST]
She is dead. [PRESENT - contamination]
We built the channel and I remember her hands. [PAST with present awareness]"
```

**Reconstructed knowledge:** More stable past tense, but with analytical present
```
"Genesis says the water prevailed [PAST - textual].
The Hebrew word is mabbul [PRESENT - analytical].
She would have experienced [CONDITIONAL - reconstruction]..."
```

**Awan's stories:** Past tense narration about past tense events (double-past)
```
"Awan told me [PAST] that her mother taught her [PAST-IN-PAST] to read stars."
```

### Quotation Marks Signal Reliability

**Direct quotes** = he's confident in exact wording (usually Awan's stories):
```
She said, "The water needs to move at walking speed."
```

**Biblical quotes** = textual precision, hostile source:
```
Genesis says, "And all flesh died that moved upon the earth."
```

**Indirect quotes** = he's paraphrasing/uncertain:
```
She said something about water speed, about walking pace, I think.
```

**No quotes** = his interpretation/summary:
```
She explained the hydraulic principles involved.
```

### White Space Usage

**Between story types** = cognitive reset needed:
```
[Reconstructed knowledge fragment]

[blank line]

[Return to pre-loss memory as refuge]
```

**Within same story type** = overwhelming, cannot continue:
```
[Genesis says that—]

[several blank lines]

[I'm going to try to write about the irrigation system instead]
```

## Summary: Strategic Deployment Guide

| Story Type | Reliability | Emotional Access | Structural Stability | Primary Function |
|------------|-------------|------------------|---------------------|------------------|
| Pre-loss Firsthand | High sensory detail, low emotional understanding | Contaminated by hindsight grief | Starts organized, may deteriorate | Show what was lost, demonstrate cognitive style in safe context |
| Reconstructed Knowledge | Textual/analytical, imagination intrudes | Mediated through hostile sources | Obsessively systematic, breaks down under emotion | The thing he's trying to understand but can't |
| Awan's Stories (Secondhand) | Exact words, uncertain meanings | Triple-layered (her original, his hearing, his grief) | Frozen and uncorrectable | Preservation, worldbuilding, showing interpretive gaps |

**Use pre-loss memories when:** He needs respite but you want to show everything is still poisoned

**Use reconstructed knowledge when:** Direct engagement with the flood, textual analysis, colonial critique

**Use Awan's stories when:** He needs maximum distance while still engaging with loss

**Interweave all three when:** Showing his cognitive/emotional state through structural breakdown and recovery attempts

---

## Final Note: What This Architecture Accomplishes

By treating these story types as structurally and psychologically distinct, you:

1. **Create authentic cognitive realism** - this is how trauma + autism actually processes memory and knowledge
2. **Give yourself narrative tools** - different textures for different purposes  
3. **Show character through structure** - his mind visible in the story architecture
4. **Build thematic meaning** - what he witnessed vs. what he learned vs. what was never understood
