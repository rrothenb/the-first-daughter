## Overview

This document explores how Samyaza's linguistic history and cognitive profile shape his narrative voice, with particular attention to his relationship with metaphor. Three factors converge to create a distinctive prose style: his origins in biblical Hebrew, centuries of speaking Tongva, and his autism.

---

## The Lakoff-Johnson Framework

George Lakoff and Mark Johnson's *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) argues that English is fundamentally constructed from conceptual metaphors - cognitive mappings where we understand abstract concepts through concrete, embodied experience. Examples:

- ARGUMENT IS WAR ("I attacked his position," "She shot down my argument")
- TIME IS MONEY ("spending time," "wasting hours")
- HAPPY IS UP / SAD IS DOWN ("feeling down," "spirits lifted")

These aren't poetic flourishes; they're the invisible architecture of how English speakers think and communicate. Native English speakers swim in metaphor without noticing it.

**Key insight for the project:** Samyaza's first two languages (Hebrew, Tongva) operate differently. English metaphor would be learned, not native - something he can deploy but doesn't inhabit naturally.

## Biblical Hebrew and Embodied Concreteness

Biblical Hebrew differs significantly from modern English in its relationship to metaphor:

### The Domains Haven't Separated

Where English maps from concrete to abstract domains, biblical Hebrew often doesn't make that move to abstraction in the first place:

- **Compassion/mercy** = *rachamim* (רחמים) - literally "bowels" or "womb." Compassion isn't *like* a gut feeling; compassion IS what happens in the gut.
- **Anger** = *af* (אף) - literally "nose/nostrils." Anger isn't *like* flared nostrils; the words are the same because the experience is the same.
- **Soul/life** = *nephesh* (נפש) - originally "throat" or "breath." Not a metaphor mapping breath onto life; they're unified.

### Smaller Vocabulary, More Polysemy

Biblical Hebrew has approximately 8,000 words compared to English's 170,000+. This creates:

- More words doing double/triple duty
- Meaning tied more closely to physical/sensory roots
- Less room for abstract floating signifiers

### Implications for Samyaza

His native thought patterns would be **concretely embodied** rather than abstractly metaphorical. When he describes emotional states, he'd naturally gravitate toward physical sensation and location rather than conceptual mapping.

## Translation Distortions in Genesis 1-11

English translations of the antediluvian Genesis narratives systematically distort the Hebrew in ways that matter for Samyaza's perspective. He experienced these events in the Hebrew way - unified, embodied, relational. English readers encounter something fundamentally different.

### Genesis 2:7 - The Creation of Adam

**Hebrew:** וַיִּיצֶר יְהוָה אֱלֹהִים אֶת־הָאָדָם עָפָר מִן־הָאֲדָמָה וַיִּפַּח בְּאַפָּיו נִשְׁמַת חַיִּים וַיְהִי הָאָדָם לְנֶפֶשׁ חַיָּה

**Typical English:** "And the LORD God formed man of the dust of the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life; and man became a living soul."

**What's Lost:**

| Hebrew | English | What Disappears |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| אָדָם / אֲדָמָה / אָדֹם (adam / adamah / adom) | "man" / "ground" | Invisible wordplay. Adam comes from adamah (ground/soil), both related to adom (red). English gives unrelated words. Hebrew encodes that humans ARE soil, ARE redness, ARE blood (dam). Etymology as ontology. |
| נֶפֶשׁ (nephesh) → "soul" | Platonic ghost | Nephesh means "animated, breathing, conscious being" - related to throat, breath, appetite. Physical. "Soul" carries centuries of body/spirit dualism. Hebrew says Adam became a *breathing creature*, not that he acquired an immaterial essence. |
| נִשְׁמַת (neshamah) → "breath" | Generic breathing | Neshamah is specifically the "higher" breath - the life-connection with divine. Distinct from ordinary respiration. English flattens the distinction. |
| אַף (aph) → "nostrils" | Emotional site | Aph is more frequently translated as "anger" or "wrath" elsewhere. The nose is where emotion shows (flared nostrils). God breathed into Adam's *anger-place*. English readers miss that the site of receiving divine breath is also the site of human rage. |

### Genesis 1:2 - Spirit/Wind

**Hebrew:** וְרוּחַ אֱלֹהִים מְרַחֶפֶת עַל־פְּנֵי הַמָּיִם

**English:** "And the Spirit of God moved upon the face of the waters"

The phrase *ruach elohim* can mean "Spirit of God" or "wind of God" or "great wind." Ruach encompasses wind, breath, and spirit as the same word. English forces a choice between physical (wind) and metaphysical (Spirit), but Hebrew doesn't make that separation. Was it a storm-wind or a divine presence? The Hebrew answer is yes.

### Genesis 3:7 - Knowledge of Nakedness

**Hebrew:** וַיֵּדְעוּ כִּי עֵירֻמִּם הֵם

The verb is *yada* - to know. In Hebrew thought, "knowing" isn't cognitive awareness but personal, intimate, experiential relationship. You can only "know" someone if you have a direct relationship with them.

So "they knew they were naked" isn't intellectual recognition - it's experiential, bodily, intimate knowledge. They *felt* their nakedness in a way that changed their relationship to their own bodies. English makes this sound like noticing a fact.

### Genesis 4:1 - Adam "Knew" Eve

**Hebrew:** וְהָאָדָם יָדַע אֶת־חַוָּה אִשְׁתּוֹ

The same verb *yada* appears here for sexual union. English either renders this as "knew" (which modern readers find quaint/euphemistic) or "had relations with" (which loses the epistemological claim entirely).

But calling *yada* a "euphemism" imposes English categories. Hebrew doesn't separate knowing from intimacy. Sex IS knowing. Body-knowledge and mind-knowledge are unified in the Hebrew conception. The same word describes Abraham's relationship with God and Adam's relationship with Eve - not because one is a metaphor for the other, but because intimacy is intimacy.

### Genesis 4:10 - Blood Crying from the Ground

**Hebrew:** קוֹל דְּמֵי אָחִיךָ צֹעֲקִים אֵלַי מִן־הָאֲדָמָה

"The voice of your brother's blood cries to me from the ground."

The wordplay network continues: *dam* (blood) cries from *adamah* (ground) - and both relate to *adam* (man) and *adom* (red). Abel's blood returns to the earth that humans are made of. English readers get unrelated words; Hebrew readers hear a single interconnected system of meaning.

### Genesis 6:6 - God's "Repentance"

**Hebrew:** וַיִּנָּחֶם יְהוָה

Often translated "the LORD repented" or "was sorry." But *nacham* is visceral - related to breathing heavily, sighing, the physical expression of grief or changed intention. English "repented" sounds like a change of moral judgment; Hebrew describes something closer to a heaving chest.

### The Pattern

What happens systematically in translation:

1. **Unified body-states become split** - Hebrew words encoding body + emotion + cognition as one thing become English words privileging one domain
2. **Concrete becomes abstract** - Physical locations and actions become metaphysical concepts
3. **Relational becomes informational** - Knowledge as intimacy becomes knowledge as data
4. **Etymology disappears** - The web of Hebrew root connections (adam/adamah/adom/dam) becomes isolated English words with no visible relationship
5. **Polysemy is forced into single meanings** - Ruach must be either "spirit" OR "wind" rather than both

### Implications for Samyaza

Samyaza experienced these events in the Hebrew way - unified, embodied, relational. When he tries to tell the story in English (or when readers encounter it in English), the language itself betrays the experience. 

This is part of why he might distrust metaphor as it's used in English: the translation history already demonstrates how language can be used to separate what should remain unified, to abstract what was physical, to break the relationships between words that encode relationships between things.

The translation history is itself part of the weaponization of Genesis. "Soul" meaning an immaterial essence that survives death - that's a translation artifact, not what *nephesh* meant. The theological edifice built on such translations is built on distortions of what the Hebrew actually said.

## Tongva Language Characteristics

Tongva (also called Gabrielino) is a Uto-Aztecan language historically spoken in the Los Angeles basin. It went extinct as a living language in the 1940s, with limited documentation surviving (primarily J.P. Harrington's field notes).

### Agglutinative Structure

Tongva builds words by stacking meaningful morphemes together, each piece visible and functional:

| English | Tongva approach |
|---------|-----------------|
| "my grandmother" (two words) | Single word with "my" + "grandmother" fused (*'Ashuuksanga*) |
| "un-friend-li-ness" (rare in English) | Standard word-building pattern |

**Psychological effect:** Meaning feels *assembled* and *transparent* rather than *evoked* or *associated*. You build meaning structurally rather than reaching across conceptual domains.

### SOV Word Order

Tongva uses Subject-Object-Verb order:

- English (SVO): "I see the river"
- Tongva (SOV): "I the river see"

After centuries of this pattern, residual syntax might emerge in Samyaza's English, especially in:
- Complex sentences
- Moments of emotional stress
- Formal or careful speech

### Limited Metaphor Data

Unfortunately, we don't have detailed analysis of Tongva figurative language. However, related Uto-Aztecan languages (like Hopi) do use spatial metaphors for time, so the family isn't non-metaphorical. But the agglutinative structure suggests meaning-making that's more structural than associative.

## Autism and Figurative Language

### The Research Picture

The relationship between autism and metaphor is more nuanced than stereotypes suggest:

- **Not a blanket deficit:** Recent research indicates that difficulty with figurative language correlates with overall language ability, not autism specifically. Autistic people with strong language skills can comprehend metaphors well.
  
- **Processing differences:** There's documented evidence of "literal-first" processing - the literal meaning is accessed before (not instead of) the figurative meaning. This creates a slight cognitive delay, not incomprehension.

- **Production vs. comprehension:** Metaphor *generation* shows more distinctive patterns than comprehension. Autistic adults may produce metaphors that are more idiosyncratic, more creative, or more "constructed" feeling.

- **Theory of Mind connection:** Understanding speaker intention matters for some figurative language (especially irony and sarcasm). Metaphor is less dependent on this than other forms.

### Implications for Samyaza

His autism wouldn't make him incapable of metaphor but would shape how he relates to it:

- **Comprehension:** He understands what metaphors mean after millennia of exposure
- **Native feel:** They don't arise naturally in his thinking; they feel like tools rather than native tongue
- **When he uses them:** There may be visible "seams" - analysis, qualification, or conscious deployment rather than unconscious flow

## The Neuroscience of Metaphor: Why It Matters

### Metaphor as Persuasion Technology

Meta-analyses confirm that metaphorical language is more persuasive than comparable literal language (Sopory & Dillard, 2002; Van Stee, 2018). This isn't just a stylistic observation - it reflects how metaphor operates at the neural level.

### Embodied Simulation

When you hear "grasp the idea," your motor cortex activates - the same regions that fire when you literally grasp something. This happens within 200 milliseconds, faster than conscious thought. The brain doesn't fully distinguish between metaphorical and literal action language at the initial processing level.

This mechanism is called **embodied simulation**: your brain runs a partial "rehearsal" of the physical experience that underlies the metaphor. Key findings:

- Listening to "John grasps the glass" activates hand-related areas of the motor cortex even when you're not performing any hand action
- The sentence "I grasped the idea" activates the same brain regions as literal grasping
- Even "dead" metaphors (like "breakthrough") activate sensorimotor areas within 250ms of exposure

### Why Metaphors Recruit More Brain Territory

Metaphors are neurologically powerful because they don't just activate language areas - they recruit:

- **Motor areas** (for action metaphors)
- **Sensory areas** (for texture, taste, temperature metaphors)
- **Spatial reasoning areas** (for orientation metaphors like UP/DOWN)
- **Emotional processing areas** (when metaphors carry affective content)

The message gets encoded across multiple neural systems simultaneously. This multi-system encoding makes metaphorical framing more memorable and more persuasive than literal statement.

### Neural Coupling

Research shows that metaphorical understanding involves neural coupling - synchronization of brain activity between speaker and listener. When metaphorical understanding occurs, brains literally sync together in ways that literal language doesn't achieve as effectively. This may explain why shared metaphorical frameworks create such strong group cohesion.

### The Familiarity Gradient

The degree of sensorimotor activation varies with metaphor familiarity:

| Metaphor Type | Neural Response |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Novel metaphors | Heavy recruitment of primary motor cortex |
| Familiar metaphors | Activation shifts to higher-order association areas |
| Dead metaphors/idioms | Minimal but still detectable sensorimotor activation |

**Implication:** Fresh metaphors hit harder neurologically. Conventional metaphors still work but more weakly. This is why effective propaganda often introduces new metaphorical frames rather than relying on tired ones.

### Metaphor as Bypass Mechanism

The neurological picture suggests that metaphor works partly by **bypassing critical evaluation**. Abstract ideas routed through embodied experience feel intuitively true because they activate the same circuits as direct physical experience. You don't evaluate "crime is a virus" the same way you'd evaluate a policy argument - it *feels* true at a bodily level before you can think about whether it makes sense.

This is why metaphorical framing has measurable effects on policy preferences. "Crime is a virus" leads people toward different solutions than "crime is a beast" - not because they've reasoned through the implications, but because different bodily intuitions get activated.

### Implications for Samyaza

This neuroscience has profound implications for Samyaza as both writer and observer:

**Partial Immunity:**
If Samyaza doesn't naturally think in metaphor, he's partly immune to this mechanism - or at least experiences it differently. His literal-first processing means:

- He may notice metaphors as *attempts to do something to him* rather than simply absorbing them
- He's less susceptible to framing effects that metaphors create
- He can observe the mechanism working on others while remaining somewhat outside it

**The Observer Advantage:**
Samyaza's cognitive style makes him a better observer of HOW metaphor works on humans, precisely because it doesn't work on him the same way. He can see the technology where others just feel the effect.

**Strategic Deployment:**
When Samyaza *does* use metaphor deliberately, he's consciously deploying a persuasion technology he understands from the outside. This might manifest as:

- More calculated selection of metaphors
- Awareness of what he's trying to make the reader feel/think
- Possible discomfort with using a mechanism he finds somewhat manipulative

**Connection to Narrative Weaponization:**
This connects directly to the project's concern with how Genesis was weaponized. Metaphor isn't decoration - it's a technology for bypassing critical thinking by routing abstract ideas through bodily experience. The same mechanism that makes "God is a father" feel intuitively true is the mechanism that makes propaganda effective.

Samyaza's ability to see metaphor as technology rather than just experiencing its effects positions him to expose how narrative manipulation works. His literal-mindedness becomes an analytical advantage.

## The Combined Effect: Samyaza's Prose Style

### Primary Characteristics

1. **Physical precision over conceptual mapping**
   - Describes bodily sensations directly rather than through metaphorical frames
   - Locates emotions in the body with unusual specificity
   - Attends to muscular states, breathing, proprioception

2. **Transparent construction**
   - Meaning is built and shown rather than evoked
   - Sentences may feel assembled, structural
   - Announces what he's doing ("I will tell you now about...")

3. **Occasional syntax residue**
   - Object-before-verb constructions in complex sentences
   - Verb-final patterns under stress or in formal register
   - Structure that feels "translated" even when it's grammatically correct

4. **Strategic metaphor deployment**
   - When metaphors appear, they may feel deliberate
   - May analyze or qualify metaphors even while using them
   - Shows awareness that metaphor is a tool for communication rather than natural thought

5. **Literal-first expressions**
   - May describe literal reality and then note the metaphorical convention
   - Precision that reads as deliberate choice rather than deficit
   - Occasional "over-literal" moments that reveal different cognitive mapping

## Concrete Examples

### Describing Grief

**Neurotypical English narrator:**
> Grief crashed over me like a wave, drowning everything in its wake. I was lost at sea, unable to find shore.

**Samyaza:**
> The grief was heavy. It sat in my chest and made breathing difficult. I could not think past it to what I should do next.

*Note: Physical, specific, located in the body. No mapping onto water/drowning domain.*

---

### Describing Loss/Absence

**Neurotypical narrator:**
> She was the light of my life, and when she left, everything went dark.

**Samyaza:**
> When she was present, I understood what to do. When she was not, I did not.

*Note: Same functional meaning (orientation/disorientation) without light/dark metaphor system.*

---

### Strategic Metaphor Deployment

**Samyaza analyzing a metaphor while using it:**
> Humans describe grief as drowning. I have drowned. They are not the same. But I understand why they reach for that word. There is the same inability to breathe. The same loss of orientation. The same irrelevance of struggle.

*Note: He's showing his work - examining why the metaphor exists rather than simply inhabiting it.*

---

### Metaphor with Immediate Qualification

> She was - I am told this is how one says it - the love of my life. Although "of my life" suggests a portion, and she was not a portion. She was the structure that made the portions intelligible.

*Note: Uses the conventional phrase, flags it as conventional, then replaces it with something more precise.*

---

### Physical State Description

**Neurotypical narrator:**
> I felt a lump in my throat.

**Samyaza:**
> My throat tightened. The muscles at the back of my jaw contracted. These are the signs that I am about to feel something I do not want to feel.

*Note: Describes literal muscular experience rather than using the "lump" metaphor. Also shows his learned pattern-recognition of his own emotional responses.*

---

### Residual Syntax Patterns

**Object-before-verb:**
> What she wanted, I could not give.

**Announcing structure:**
> The thing that happened next, I will tell you now.

**Verb-final under stress:**
> Everything I had built, everything I had believed, in that moment undone was.

## Voice Variations by Context

### When Describing Awan

Likely his most emotionally charged material. May show:
- Greater physical precision (attending to small details of her body, movement, voice)
- More struggle between literal description and reaching for adequate language
- Possible moments where he tries metaphor and abandons it
- Hebrew-rooted language for intimacy and embodiment

### When Narrating Violence

May become very clinical and precise:
- Physical mechanics described without emotional mapping
- Contrast between his detached observation and the horror of content
- Possible dissociative quality that reflects both autism and trauma

### When Explaining Technical Concepts

Most comfortable register:
- Clear structural building of ideas
- Definitions before deployment
- Pattern-based reasoning visible
- May be where his natural voice feels most fluent

### When Addressing the Reader Directly

Meta-awareness of the writing project itself:
- Explicit acknowledgment of language as a tool/problem
- Discussion of translation difficulties
- Analysis of how stories work and fail
- This is where strategic metaphor use might be most visible

## Practical Guidelines for Writing Samyaza

### Do:

- Ground emotional descriptions in physical sensation
- Use precise, specific language over evocative vagueness
- Let him analyze his own word choices occasionally
- Allow residual syntax patterns in complex or emotional passages
- Show him reaching for and sometimes rejecting metaphors
- Let him notice patterns (in himself, in language, in others)

### Avoid:

- Long chains of unconscious metaphor
- Flowery or "poetic" language that he wouldn't naturally produce
- Emotional descriptions that float free of the body
- Idioms used without awareness that they're idiomatic
- Standard narrative voice patterns that assume neurotypical cognition

### Remember:

His voice should feel *precise* and *constructed* without feeling *cold* or *robotic*. The precision comes from a different relationship to language, not from lack of feeling. If anything, the precision exists because he's trying very hard to communicate experiences that matter deeply to him, and he doesn't trust the easy shortcuts of conventional language to do the work.
